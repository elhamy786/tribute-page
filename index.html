Dr. Norman Borlaug

Dr. Norman Borlaug, American agronomist and humanitarian.

Dr. Norman Borlaug (1914-2009) was an American agronomist known as the "father of the Green Revolution." He developed high-yield, disease-resistant wheat varieties and promoted agricultural innovations in developing countries, significantly increasing food production and alleviating hunger. His work earned him the Nobel Peace Prize in 1970.

Through his work, he helped to alleviate global hunger and save millions of lives.

Dr. Norman Borlaug was a key figure in the 20th-century agricultural transformation known as the Green Revolution. Born in 1914 in Iowa, he earned a Ph.D. in plant pathology and genetics from the University of Minnesota. Borlaug's most notable achievement was developing high-yield, disease-resistant wheat varieties, which he introduced in Mexico, India, and Pakistan during the mid-20th century. These innovations greatly increased food production and helped prevent famines, saving millions of lives. In recognition of his contributions to world peace through increasing food security, he was awarded the Nobel Peace Prize in 1970. Borlaug's legacy continues to influence agricultural practices and food policies worldwide.
